**Inheriting a house in Detroit is rarely simple, but you have more options than you think.** Between probate, back taxes, repairs, and other heirs, it can feel like a maze. Here is how it actually works in Wayne County, and how selling as-is for cash can be the clean way out.

## How probate works in Michigan

When someone passes and the house was in their name alone, it usually has to go through probate before it can be sold. The court appoints a personal representative, who then has the authority to handle and sell the property.

Michigan probate typically runs seven to 12 months. The good news is you do not always have to wait for the whole thing to finish. In many cases a personal representative can line up and negotiate a sale while the process is still moving. And if the estate is small, or the home was held in a trust or with a survivorship deed, you may be able to avoid full probate altogether.

## The 42-day clock most heirs do not know about

This is the part that catches families off guard. Under Michigan law, if no one opens a probate estate within **42 days** of the death, a creditor, including a mortgage lender, can petition the court to appoint its own personal representative.

That person does not work for your family. Their job is to protect the lender, which can mean a rushed sale that covers the mortgage and leaves the heirs with little or nothing. Opening the estate early, or selling before it gets to that point, keeps control where it belongs.

## Watch the property taxes after transfer

When ownership changes, Michigan's Proposal A cap comes off and the taxable value can uncap to the current state equalized value. For a house that has been in the family for decades, that can double or even triple the annual tax bill.

So the longer an inherited house sits, the more it can cost you, on top of insurance, utilities, upkeep, and the risk of vandalism on a vacant property. Those holding costs add up fast.

## When more than one person inherits

If you and your siblings or relatives inherited the house together, all of you generally have to agree before it can be sold. When heirs cannot agree, any one of them can file a partition action and ask the court to force a sale.

A clean cash sale is often the simplest way through this. It turns the house into cash that splits evenly, avoids a drawn-out partition fight, and closes in days instead of months.
