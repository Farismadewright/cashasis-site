**If you are done being a Detroit landlord, you are not alone, and you are not stuck.** Between the Certificate of Compliance rules, blight tickets, rent escrow, and the constant repairs, Detroit has made small-time landlording a grind. ## Detroit's rental rules are stacked against small landlords

To legally rent a property in Detroit, you need a Certificate of Compliance, which means registering with the city and passing inspection. The catch is that almost nobody has one. Only around 14 percent of Detroit's roughly 82,000 rentals are compliant.

And here is the part that bites: it is actually illegal to collect rent during any period the property does not have a valid Certificate of Compliance. So a huge number of Detroit landlords are technically operating against the rules, racking up exposure every month.

## The tickets, liens, and escrow add up fast

Failing to register or comply does not just sit quietly. It turns into:

- **Blight tickets.** Failure to register a rental is treated as a blight violation, and the city enforces these aggressively.
- **Liens.** The city now has the power to place liens on properties when owners do not pay their fines, and collections have jumped because of it.
- **Rent escrow.** A tenant in a property without a Certificate of Compliance can legally put their rent into an escrow account instead of paying you. If you do not get compliant in time, that money goes to the tenant, not you.

So you can end up unable to collect rent, piling up fines, and facing liens, all on a property that is already costing you in repairs.

## Selling does not just pass the problem along

You might think you can quietly sell and hand the headache to the next owner. It does not work that cleanly. When a Detroit rental is sold, the new owner has to register and get their own Certificate of Compliance, and transferring the property does not erase fines that were assessed while you owned it.
