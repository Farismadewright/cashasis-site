**Divorce is hard enough without a fight over the house.** In Michigan, the marital home is often the biggest shared asset and the most emotional one. The good news is that a clean sale can let both people split fairly and move on, without dragging it out. Here is how it works in Detroit.

## How Michigan divides the home

Michigan is an equitable distribution state, not a community property state. That means the house is not automatically cut in half. Instead, the court aims for a division that is fair given the circumstances of the marriage, which sometimes is fifty-fifty and sometimes is not.

One thing surprises a lot of people: whose name is on the deed usually does not decide it. A home bought during the marriage is generally treated as marital property regardless of the title. So both spouses typically have a stake in the equity.

## Your three options for the house

When it comes to the actual house, divorcing couples in Michigan usually pick one of three paths:

1. **One spouse buys out the other.** The keeping spouse pays the other their share of the equity, usually by refinancing the loan into their own name alone. This only works if that spouse can qualify and afford it.
2. **Hold it and sell later.** Sometimes one spouse stays for a while, often for the kids, then the house sells down the road.
3. **Sell now and split the proceeds.** The house is sold, the mortgage is paid off, and both walk away with their share.

For many couples, the third option is the cleanest.

## Why a clean sale is often the simplest

Selling the house turns an emotional, illiquid asset into cash both people can actually use. It means neither spouse stays financially tied to the other through the property, and the split becomes simple math once it closes.

There is also a mortgage trap worth knowing. A divorce judgment does not bind your lender. If both names are on the loan and one person is supposed to pay but does not, the bank can still come after the other and the credit damage is real. Selling the house pays off the loan and removes both names, which fully separates you.

And if the two of you cannot agree on what to do with the home, a Michigan court can order it sold anyway and the proceeds divided. Choosing to sell on your own terms beats having that decision made for you.
