**A fire-damaged house in Detroit does not have to become a months-long rebuild.** You can sell it as-is, for cash, skip the contractors and permits, and in many cases keep your insurance payout. Here is how it works in Michigan.

## You can sell as-is, no repairs required

In Michigan, it is completely legal to sell a fire-damaged house without fixing a thing, as long as you are honest about its condition. You do not have to restore it, clean it out, or get it inspected first. You sell it in the state it is in.

That matters because fire repairs are brutal. They mean managing contractors, pulling permits, sitting through repeated inspections, and dealing with the hidden damage fires leave behind, like weakened beams and cracked walls you cannot see. All while you keep paying taxes, insurance, and maybe a mortgage on a house you cannot live in.

## The one rule: disclosure

Michigan does require a Seller's Disclosure Statement that lists known material defects, and fire damage is one of them. You disclose what you know. Selling as-is does not erase that requirement.

But here is the thing. When you sell to a buyer who is purchasing the house specifically because of its condition, the fire is not a problem to hide. It is just documentation. We already know the house had a fire, that is why we are buying it, so the disclosure is a formality, not a dealbreaker the way it might be with a retail buyer.

## You can keep the insurance money

This is the part many owners do not realize. You can often sell the house as-is and keep your insurance payout instead of pouring it into a rebuild.

If your claim is already paid, in most cases that money is yours, and you can sell the damaged house on top of it. If the claim is still open, the proceeds can be factored into the sale or assigned at closing. Either way, you do not have to spend the payout rebuilding. Some owners take the check, sell the house as-is, and walk away with both.

## Why selling beats rebuilding

Rebuilding a fire-damaged house is expensive, slow, and uncertain. Even after you spend the time and money, there is no guarantee you recoup it, and you still have to disclose the past fire to any future buyer.

Selling as-is skips all of that. No repair costs, no waiting on contractors, no living next to the daily reminder of the fire. For a lot of people, the clean break is worth more than chasing a full restoration.
