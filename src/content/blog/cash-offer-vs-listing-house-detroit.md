**There is no single right answer to cash offer versus listing. There is only the right answer for your house and your situation.** The honest way to decide is to look at the full math, not just the sticker price. Here is how the two stack up for a Detroit seller.

## What listing actually looks like

Listing with an agent can get you a higher asking price, and for a home in good condition that is often the right call. But the sticker price is not what you keep. With a listing you typically deal with:

- **Agent commission** taken out of the sale.
- **Repairs and prep** to get the house ready to show and to pass a buyer's inspection.
- **Time.** In Detroit, selling on the open market commonly runs around three months from listing to closing, sometimes longer.
- **Financing risk.** Most buyers use a mortgage, and a deal can fall apart at appraisal or underwriting, sending you back to square one.

If your house shows well and you can wait, listing can net you more. That is the honest case for it.

## What a cash sale looks like

A cash offer is lower on the sticker, but it strips out almost everything that eats into a listing:

- **No commission.**
- **No repairs.** You sell as-is, in any condition.
- **No staging or showings.**
- **No financing falling through**, because the money is not waiting on a bank.
- **Fast close**, often in as little as a week, on your timeline.

So the real comparison is not list price versus cash price. It is what lands in your pocket after commission, repairs, and months of holding costs, against a clean number you can count on.

## Why condition tips the scale in Detroit

This is where Detroit matters specifically. A lot of the city's housing is older and needs work. When a house needs repairs, a buyer using an FHA or conventional loan often gets the appraisal flagged, which means you either fix it or watch the deal stall.

For a dated, damaged, or distressed Detroit house, the gap between a listing and a cash offer narrows fast once you subtract repair costs, commission, and the months of taxes and insurance you pay while it sits. In those cases, the cash route often nets close to the same with far less hassle and risk.

## When each one wins

Here is the straight version:

1. **List it** if the house is in good shape, you can wait two to three months, and you are willing to pay commission and do prep work to chase the highest price.
2. **Take a cash offer** if the house needs work, you need speed or certainty, you are dealing with taxes, probate, a tenant, or a vacant property, or you simply do not want the hassle of a traditional sale.

Neither is automatically better. It comes down to your house, your timeline, and what you actually want to net.
